welcome back to another episode of "why do i even play this game." today we’re looking at a game that is, quite frankly, a triumph of the human spirit. not because of the brilliant tactical shots or the deep strategic maneuvers, but because of the sheer, unadulterated commitment to doing absolutely nothing until the clock literally stopped ticking.

we’re talking about game qR4nnL9t on lichess. a correspondence game. for those of you who don’t know, correspondence chess is the version of chess where you have days to think about a single move. it’s designed for people who want to find the absolute truth of a position, or more accurately, for people who want to feel guilty about not moving for forty-eight hours straight.

this particular game featured white (oslab, rated 1832) against black (woaster, rated 1637). on paper, oslab is the favorite. in practice, time was the winner.

### the opening: a cold shower

the game started with 1. e4. a classic. white wants to control the center. white wants a fight. black responds with 1... c6. the caro-kann. the "i’m not here to have fun, i’m here to suffer and eventually win an endgame" defense. it’s the chess equivalent of wearing a beige cardigan to a heavy metal concert. it’s solid, it’s respectable, and it’s incredibly annoying to play against.

![the calm before the storm](https://lichess1.org/export/fen.png?fen=r1b1kbnr/pp2pppp/2n5/3p4/3P4/5N2/PPP2PPP/RNBQKB1R%20w%20KQkq%20-%201%205)

then white plays 2. nf3. wait, what? this isn’t the main line. usually, you see 2. d4. oslab is already playing mind games. is it a two knights? is it an invitation to some obscure gambit? black doesn’t care. black plays 2... d5. the caro-kann must be the caro-kann.

3. exd5 cxd5. we’ve reached the exchange variation. symmetry has been achieved. the tension has been released. at this point, most players are already thinking about what they’re going to have for lunch because the position is as flat as a pancake that’s been run over by a steamroller.

4. d4 nc6. okay, we’re developing pieces. we’re following the principles. everything is normal. the engine says it’s 0.00. the gods of chess are yawning.

### the "climax"

![the check that changed nothing](https://lichess1.org/export/fen.png?fen=r1b1kbnr/pp2pppp/2n5/qB1p4/3P4/5N2/PPP2PPP/RNBQK2R%20w%20KQkq%20-%202%206)

and then, move 5. bb5. oslab decides to pin the knight. it’s a standard idea, but in this specific setup, it feels like white is trying to start a fight in a library. "i’m pinning your knight, woaster! what are you going to do about it?"

woaster, probably feeling a sudden surge of adrenaline that hasn’t been seen in a caro-kann since the mid-90s, plays 5... qa5+. a check! a double attack! the queen is out, the king is in check, and the bishop on b5 is hanging. it’s a "gotcha" move. it’s the kind of move you play when you want to look like a tactical genius for exactly one second.

white, completely unfazed, plays 6. nc3. 

![the absolute defense](https://lichess1.org/export/fen.png?fen=r1b1kbnr/pp2pppp/2n5/qB1p4/3P4/2N2N2/PPP2PPP/R1BQK2R%20b%20KQkq%20-%203%206)

and that’s it. that’s the whole game.

white develops a piece, defends the bishop, blocks the check, and maintains a solid center. it’s the most logical move in the world. the engine still says it’s roughly equal, maybe a tiny bit better for white because of the space and the development.

but for black, this was apparently the end of the world.

### the long silence

now, remember, this is correspondence chess. 2 days per move. woaster had 48 hours to look at 6. nc3 and realize that, while the "brilliant" queen check didn’t immediately win the game, the position is still perfectly playable. you just play e6, or nf6, or maybe even g6 if you’re feeling spicy. you keep playing. you have days to find a plan.

but no. 

apparently, move 6. nc3 was so psychologically devastating, so profoundly unexpected, that woaster simply ceased to exist. 

did woaster fall into a deep meditative trance, searching for a way to refute the existence of the knight on c3? did they embark on a quest to the Himalayas to ask a wise monk how to handle a hanging bishop that is no longer hanging? or did they, more likely, just forget they had a game going until the notification popped up saying "game over - white wins on time"?

there is something uniquely tragic about losing a correspondence game on move 6 by time forfeit. it’s not like a blitz game where you hang your queen because your cat jumped on your keyboard. in correspondence, you have to actively decide to ignore your responsibilities for days. you have to see the "it's your turn" notification and think, "not today, satan. not today."

### a critical analysis of the "strategy"

let’s be critical for a moment. the move 5... qa5+ is a bit of a "hope chess" move. you’re hoping your opponent is an idiot and doesn’t see that the bishop is hanging, or you’re hoping they don’t know how to block a check while defending a piece. but oslab is rated 1832. they aren’t going to just give you a bishop for free on move 6. 

by playing qa5+, black committed the cardinal sin of bringing the queen out too early without a clear follow-up. and when white replied with the simplest move in the book, black’s entire "system" collapsed. it wasn’t a tactical collapse; it was a moral one.

the time forfeit is the ultimate admission of defeat. it says, "i tried to be clever, it didn't work immediately, and now i am too embarrassed to continue." it’s the chess equivalent of throwing a smoke bomb to exit a room, only for the smoke to clear and reveal you’re still standing there, and the door is locked.

### conclusion

so, what can we learn from game qR4nnL9t?

1. don't play 5... qa5+ if you aren't prepared for your opponent to play the most obvious move in response.
2. if you're playing correspondence, maybe set an alarm.
3. the caro-kann is a solid opening, but it can't protect you from yourself.

in the end, oslab walked away with 4 rating points for doing absolutely nothing other than knowing how to block a check. and woaster? woaster left us with a masterpiece of brevity and procrastination. a game that lasted 6 moves in notation, but probably felt like an eternity of shame in real time.

see you next time, and remember: even if you suck at chess, at least try to move your pieces before the sun burns out.
