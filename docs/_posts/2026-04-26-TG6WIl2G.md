There is a specific kind of pain that only a chess player can understand. It’s not the pain of losing a hard-fought battle against a grandmaster. It’s not even the pain of blundering your Queen on move 5 because you were distracted by a particularly interesting pigeon outside your window. No, the true, soul-crushing agony comes from reaching a position of absolute, unadulterated dominance and then—for reasons that probably require a team of neurologists to explain—deciding that you’ve had enough of winning and would much rather experience the cold, bitter embrace of defeat.

Welcome to my latest masterpiece. A game so baffling, so fundamentally broken, that it makes "The Room" look like "Citizen Kane." 

I was playing White. My opponent? Lichess AI Level 3. For those who don't know, AI Level 3 is essentially a calculator that has been dropped in a bathtub. it knows how the pieces move, but its tactical awareness is roughly on par with a head of lettuce. And yet, somehow, I managed to snatch defeat from the jaws of a victory so certain it was practically pre-ordained.

### The "London" But Worse

I started with **1. d4**. A solid move. A respectable move. Then came **1... d5 2. c3**. I was going for something London-ish, or maybe just something where I didn't have to think too hard. My opponent played **2... e6**, and I followed up with **3. Bf4**. 

At this point, I felt like Magnus Carlsen. I was developing pieces. I was controlling the center. I was a god among men. My opponent played **3... c5**, challenging the center, and I played **4. e3**. 

Everything was going according to plan. Then Black played **4... Qb6**, attacking my b2 pawn. This is the oldest trick in the book. It’s the kind of move a toddler makes when they want your attention. And how did I respond? With the clinical precision of a master?

**5. Qa4+?**

Why? I don't know. I wanted to check the King? The King isn't even bothered. Black just plays **5... Bd7**, developing a piece and attacking my Queen. Now I have to move the Queen again. I play **6. Qb3**, offering a trade. Black accepts: **6... Qxb3 7. axb3**.

Now, look at the board. I have doubled pawns on the b-file, but I have a nice open a-file for my Rook. I’m doing okay. I’m still a god. A slightly confused god, but a god nonetheless.

### The Gift That Kept On Giving

Then, Black played **7... c4**. 

I want you to pause for a second and appreciate this move. Black just pushed a pawn into a square where it can be taken by two different pawns. It’s not a gambit. It’s not a deep positional sacrifice. It’s a cry for help.

Naturally, I took it: **8. bxc4**. Thank you, Mr. AI. I’ll take that free pawn. 

Black then played **8... h6**. Another masterstroke. Just a casual pawn move while I’m preparing to dismantle their entire existence. I played **9. Na3**, developing my Knight and preparing to jump into the heart of Black’s position. Black played **9... Be7**, and I saw my opportunity.

**10. Nb5!**

The threat is simple: Nc7+ forking the King and Rook. It’s the kind of threat that even a Level 3 AI should see. And how did Black respond? **10... Kf8**. 

Black just... moved the King. They gave up the right to castle and blocked their own Rook, all to avoid a fork that I was still going to execute anyway. At this point, I wasn't just winning; I was bullying a machine.

### The Siege of the AI

I continued my development: **11. Nf3 a6 12. Nc7**. There it is. The Knight is on c7, staring down the Rook. Black played **12... e5**, a desperate attempt to create some space. I took it with the Knight: **13. Nxe5**. 

Now I have a Knight on e5 and a Knight on c7. Black’s Rook is trapped on a8. Black played **13... Ra7**, trying to save the Rook, but it’s now stuck in a corner like a naughty child.

I played **14. Bd3**, finishing my development. Black played **14... h5**, and I started pushing my central pawns: **15. cxd5 h4 16. d6**. 

Look at that d6 pawn. It’s a monster. It’s a shark in a swimming pool. Black played **16... Bd8**, trying to stop it, but the damage was done. I followed up with **17. Bc4**, putting more pressure on the f7 square. Black played **17... g6**, and then came the hammer:

**18. Nxf7!**

Forking the Rook on h8 and the Bishop on d8 (well, the Bishop moved to f6 later). Black played **18... Bf6**. 

I was up material. I had a better position. I had the AI on the ropes. I decided to go for the kill.

**19. Ne6+?**

Wait, why did I do that? I just gave away a Knight for a King move. Black took it: **19... Kxf7**. 

Okay, fine. I still have a Knight on f7... wait, no, I had two Knights? No, I had one on e5 and one on c7. Let's re-examine my life choices.

**20. Ng5+!** 

Double check? No, just a check. Black played **20... Kg7**.

### The Great Resignation

And this, dear reader, is where the story takes a turn for the surreal. 

I am White. I am up a piece (if you count the fact that Black’s Rook on a7 is effectively a paperweight). I have two incredibly strong bishops. I have a Knight on g5 that is annoying as hell. And I have two passed pawns on the d-file that are about to become Queens.

The engine evaluation at this point is probably somewhere around +10. It’s not just a winning position; it’s a "you-can-play-this-blindfolded-while-juggling-chainsaws" winning position.

And what did I do? 

I resigned.

I didn't lose on time. I didn't have a power outage. I didn't have a sudden realization that I had left the oven on. I simply looked at the board, decided that I had seen enough, and clicked the little white flag.

Why? 

Maybe it was a misclick. Maybe my mouse decided to rebel against its master. Or maybe, just maybe, I realized that I didn't deserve to win. I had played a game so messy, so full of weird Queen moves and unnecessary Knight jumps, that the only honorable thing to do was to give the win to the AI.

After all, the AI didn't resign. It kept playing. It played badly, sure, but it played. It had more dignity in its silicon chips than I had in my entire body.

### A Critical Self-Reflection

What can we learn from game TG6WIl2G? 

1. **Don't play correspondence chess while sleep-deprived.** Your brain will start seeing ghosts and you'll end up resigning positions that a goldfish could win.
2. **The London System is a gateway drug.** It starts with a solid opening and ends with you questioning your entire existence.
3. **Respect the AI.** Even Level 3. It might not be smart, but it’s persistent. And sometimes, persistence is all you need to win against a human who has lost their mind.

I’m going to take a break from chess for a while. I’m going to go outside, breathe some fresh air, and try to forget that I ever played this game. If you see me on Lichess, please, don't challenge me. I’ll probably just resign on move 1 out of habit.
