Some people play chess to exercise their minds, to engage in a battle of wits, or to enjoy the sheer aesthetic beauty of a well-coordinated attack. I, apparently, play chess to see how quickly I can dismantle my own dignity. 

Today we are looking at a game I played recently—though calling it a "game" feels like calling a 12-car pileup a "commute." If you’ve ever wondered what it looks like when a human brain decides to take a coffee break in the middle of a blitz game, look no further than my encounter with `akram908`.

### The Opening: Innocence and Ignorance

I was White. I felt good. I had my 1044 rating points, which in the grand scheme of things means I know how the horse moves but still occasionally try to jump over my own pieces. I opened with **1. e4**. A classic. A statement. "I am going to control the center," I told myself.

`akram908` responded with **1... d5**, the Scandinavian Defense. Now, the Scandinavian is a perfectly respectable opening. It’s direct, it challenges the center immediately, and it usually leads to some interesting positions. A normal person would play `2. exd5`. 

I am not a normal person.

I played **2. f3**.

If you heard a faint screaming sound just now, that was every chess grandmaster in a five-mile radius. **2. f3** is the kind of move you make when you accidentally click the wrong square but are too proud to admit it. It’s slow, it weakens the king’s side, and it does absolutely nothing to address the tension in the center. It’s the chess equivalent of bringing a wet noodle to a sword fight.

My opponent, sensing that they were playing against a literal potato, took the pawn: **2... dxe4**. I recaptured with **3. fxe4**. At this point, I had successfully opened the f-file for no reason and made my king feel extremely vulnerable. Progress!

### The Development: A False Sense of Security

For the next few moves, things actually looked... okay? We developed some pieces. **3... Nc6 4. Nc3 e5 5. Nf3 Nf6 6. d3**. I was building a solid structure. My pawns were there. My knights were out. I was feeling like maybe, just maybe, I could overcome the tactical disaster of move 2.

Then came **6... Bc5**. An annoying move. It pins the d-pawn (well, not really a pin, but it eyes the f2 square and makes castling difficult). I responded with **7. Bg5**, trying to be aggressive. **7... h6 8. Bh4 g5**. 

Ah, the classic "I’m going to kick your bishop" move. Usually, players at my level do this and then realize they’ve weakened their king. But in this game, it didn't matter because I was about to do something much, much worse.

I retreated: **9. Bg3**. They followed up with **9... Bg4**. Now we had a mirror situation. My knight on f3 was pinned. I tried to kick their bishop: **10. h3 Bh5**. 

And here is where the wheels didn't just fall off the wagon—the wagon exploded, and the horses went on strike.

### The Meltdown: 11. d4??

I looked at the board. I saw their knight on c6. I saw my bishop on g3. I thought, "I need to open things up." 

I played **11. d4**.

This move is a masterpiece of incompetence. It’s a central pawn push that literally gives away a pawn for nothing. **11... Nxd4** was the obvious reply. Now my knight on f3 is under even more pressure.

But wait, it gets better. Instead of trying to defend or minimize the damage, I decided to go for a "tactical" shot.

**12. Nxe5??**

I would love to tell you what I was thinking. I really would. Maybe I thought I was winning a pawn? Maybe I thought I was threatening something on f7? 

In reality, I simply forgot that my Queen was on d1.

`akram908` did not forget. **12... Bxd1**. 

There it goes. My Queen. The most powerful piece on the board. Traded for... nothing. Actually, not even traded. Just donated. It was a charitable contribution to the `akram908` Victory Fund.

### The Aftermath: Playing for the Gallery

At this point, a sensible person would resign. But I have two things that prevent me from being sensible: a stubborn streak and a misplaced hope that my opponent might also have a stroke.

**13. Rxd1**. I took back a piece. Yay? 
**13... Nxc2+**. A fork. Because of course.
**14. Ke2**. My king begins his long, lonely walk.
**14... Nd4+**. Another check.
**15. Rxd4**. I am so desperate that I trade my Rook for a Knight. At this point, the material advantage is so heavily in Black's favor that the engine isn't even giving a number anymore; it's just laughing in binary.

**15... Bxd4**. 
**16. Nxf7??** Another brilliant move. I'm attacking the Rook and the King! Surely this will save me!
**16... Kxf7**. No. It just loses another piece.

### The King’s Final Journey

The rest of the game is essentially a horror movie where the monster (Black’s army) slowly corners the final girl (my King).

**17. Nd5 Nxd5 18. exd5 Re8+ 19. Kd1 Qxd5**. 
The Black Queen is now central and terrifying. My king is shivering in the middle of the board like a wet chihuahua.

**20. b3**. I try to create some breathing room.
**20... Be3+**. Check.
**21. Ke2**. Oh, look, my king is moving forward again. That's always a good sign in the endgame, right? 
Except it's not the endgame. It's the middle-game, and I'm missing half my pieces.

**21... Qd2+**.
**22. Kf3**. The king is now on f3. He’s out for a stroll. He’s taking in the fresh air of the center-right of the board.
**22... Kg6**. Black is bringing their king into the action? No, they’re just preparing the final blow.

**23. Bb5**. I develop my bishop! At move 23! Who says I'm not a positional genius?
**23... Re7**. 
**24. Re1**. I’m "defending" the bishop.
**24... Rf8+**. 
**25. Kg4**. My king has reached g4. He has traveled all the way from e1 to g4. He has seen the world. He has met interesting people. 

And now, he meets his end.

**25... h5#**

Checkmate.

### Conclusion: Lessons Learned?

What can we learn from this game? 

1. **Don't play 2. f3.** Just... don't.
2. **Check if your Queen is hanging.** This seems basic, but as I’ve demonstrated, it’s a lesson I still haven't fully internalized.
3. **If you lose your Queen on move 12, maybe just go get a sandwich instead of playing it out.**

`akram908` played a solid game, taking advantage of every single one of my numerous, glaring mistakes. As for me? I’m going to go study some basic tactics. Or maybe I’ll just take up checkers. Do they have Queens in checkers? They have Kings. I’m already great at moving my King.
